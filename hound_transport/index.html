§hound-transport

The IO shell that moves the native engine’s encoded media over the wire. The wire is a MoQ session (moq-net) carried on a raw-QUIC iroh connection (web-transport-iroh) over the iroh Endpoint the app already owns.

§Why MoQ, and what it buys us

Media-over-QUIC gives us a group/object model that maps cleanly onto encoded video and lets QUIC — not hand-rolled application code — own framing and loss recovery. The mapping is the whole idea:

  • one media flow → one MoQ track (the unit you subscribe to),
  • one GOP (keyframe + its deltas) → one group, carried on its own QUIC uni-stream,
  • one encoded frame → one object, an opaque payload forwarded byte-for-byte.

Two properties fall out of that, and they are why the design is shaped this way:

  • Within a GOP, the uni-stream is ordered and reliable, so a lost packet is repaired by QUIC’s own retransmit — there is no application-level NACK on the video path.
  • Across GOPs, each group is an independent stream, so an older GOP whose stream has stalled can never head-of-line-block a newer keyframe. The subscriber drains with next_group’s priority-drop: a stale group is discarded at its boundary rather than waited on. That is the direct cure for the classic “a keyframe queued behind a backlog of deltas freezes the picture” failure.

§Sans-IO discipline

This crate is only the IO shell. The pure [hound_media] cores that own timing and rate control sit directly behind it, consuming bytes + timestamps and emitting reassembled frames / playout timing — they never touch a socket:

  • jitter/playout ([hound_media::PlayoutBuffer]) consumes the reassembled [hound_media::Frame]s the subscriber hands out,
  • the A/V clock ([hound_media::CaptureClock]) supplies now_us,
  • the BWE→encoder loop ([hound_media::RateController] / [hound_media::QualityLadder]) drives the encoder that feeds the publisher,
  • AEC lives in the engine, upstream of here.

Because MoQ owns video framing and recovery, the custom packetizer/reassembler/ PacketHeader in [hound_media] are not on the video path; they remain only for the impairment simulator and any datagram experiments.

§The two connection shapes

  • Peer-to-peer (MoqPublisher / MoqSubscriber): a publisher and a subscriber endpoint speak MoQ directly over one iroh connection. Used in tests and for direct links.
  • Relayed (MoqRelaySession / MoqRelaySubscriber): the client dials the SFU relay (services/sfu), presents a space-scoped relay token, and the relay forwards every publisher’s broadcast to the other subscribers in the same space. MoqRelaySession multiplexes many tracks (every mic/cam/screen track of a call, both published and subscribed) over a SINGLE connection — see its docs for why that matters for congestion control and handshake cost.
