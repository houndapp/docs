1//! # hound-transport
2//!
3//! The **IO shell** that moves the native engine's encoded media over the wire. The
4//! wire is a **MoQ session** ([`moq-net`](moq_net)) carried on a raw-QUIC **iroh**
5//! connection (`web-transport-iroh`) over the iroh `Endpoint` the app already owns.
6//!
7//! ## Why MoQ, and what it buys us
8//!
9//! Media-over-QUIC gives us a group/object model that maps cleanly onto encoded
10//! video and lets QUIC — not hand-rolled application code — own framing and loss
11//! recovery. The mapping is the whole idea:
12//!
13//! * one media flow → one MoQ **track** (the unit you subscribe to),
14//! * one GOP (keyframe + its deltas) → one **group**, carried on its own QUIC
15//!   **uni-stream**,
16//! * one encoded frame → one **object**, an opaque payload forwarded byte-for-byte.
17//!
18//! Two properties fall out of that, and they are why the design is shaped this way:
19//!
20//! * **Within a GOP**, the uni-stream is ordered and reliable, so a lost packet is
21//!   repaired by QUIC's own retransmit — there is no application-level NACK on the
22//!   video path.
23//! * **Across GOPs**, each group is an independent stream, so an older GOP whose
24//!   stream has stalled can never head-of-line-block a newer keyframe. The
25//!   subscriber drains with `next_group`'s *priority-drop*: a stale group is
26//!   discarded at its boundary rather than waited on. That is the direct cure for
27//!   the classic "a keyframe queued behind a backlog of deltas freezes the picture"
28//!   failure.
29//!
30//! ## Sans-IO discipline
31//!
32//! This crate is **only** the IO shell. The pure [`hound_media`] cores that own
33//! timing and rate control sit directly behind it, consuming bytes + timestamps and
34//! emitting reassembled frames / playout timing — they never touch a socket:
35//!
36//! * jitter/playout ([`hound_media::PlayoutBuffer`]) consumes the reassembled
37//!   [`hound_media::Frame`]s the subscriber hands out,
38//! * the A/V clock ([`hound_media::CaptureClock`]) supplies `now_us`,
39//! * the BWE→encoder loop ([`hound_media::RateController`] /
40//!   [`hound_media::QualityLadder`]) drives the encoder that feeds the publisher,
41//! * AEC lives in the engine, upstream of here.
42//!
43//! Because MoQ owns video framing and recovery, the custom packetizer/reassembler/
44//! `PacketHeader` in [`hound_media`] are not on the video path; they remain only for
45//! the impairment simulator and any datagram experiments.
46//!
47//! ## The two connection shapes
48//!
49//! * **Peer-to-peer** ([`MoqPublisher`] / [`MoqSubscriber`]): a publisher and a
50//!   subscriber endpoint speak MoQ directly over one iroh connection. Used in tests
51//!   and for direct links.
52//! * **Relayed** ([`MoqRelaySession`] / [`MoqRelaySubscriber`]): the client dials
53//!   the SFU relay (`services/sfu`), presents a space-scoped relay token, and the
54//!   relay forwards every publisher's broadcast to the other subscribers in the same
55//!   space. [`MoqRelaySession`] multiplexes many tracks (every mic/cam/screen track
56//!   of a call, both published and subscribed) over a SINGLE connection — see its
57//!   docs for why that matters for congestion control and handshake cost.
